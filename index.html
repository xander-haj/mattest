Order Orthoptera

Grasshoppers are spring-loaded meadow engineers.

Built for jumping, tuned for vibration, and woven into grassland food webs, grasshoppers are among the most recognizable insects people meet in warm open places.

Fast signals

What makes a grasshopper a grasshopper?

01

Hind legs work like levers

Their enlarged back legs store and release force quickly, helping many species leap far beyond their body length.

02

They hear with body membranes

Many short-horned grasshoppers detect sound through tympanal organs on the sides of the abdomen.

03

Young grasshoppers molt

Nymphs resemble small adults, but they shed their outer skeleton several times before reaching full size.

Body plan

A compact machine for chewing, sensing, and jumping

Grasshoppers have the classic insect body plan: head, thorax, abdomen, six legs, antennae, and a hard external skeleton. Their chewing mouthparts are adapted for plant material, while their compound eyes help them detect motion across a wide field of view.

The thorax anchors the legs and wings. The abdomen houses major digestive and reproductive organs, and in many species it also carries the sound-sensing membranes used during courtship and escape behavior.

Antennae
Short sensory structures that sample touch and chemical cues around leaves and stems.
Pronotum
A saddle-like plate behind the head that helps protect the front of the thorax.
Femur
The thick upper segment of the hind leg where much of the jumping power is stored.

Development

From egg pod to winged adult

  1. Egg

    Females place clusters of eggs in soil or plant litter, where the eggs are buffered from weather swings and many surface predators.

  2. Nymph

    Young grasshoppers emerge without fully developed wings and feed actively as they pass through molts.

  3. Adult

    Adults can reproduce, disperse more effectively, and in many species use sound or display during courtship.

Where they live

Grasslands, edges, fields, and warm open ground

Sunlit vegetation

Many species thrive where sunlight warms the ground and grasses provide food, cover, and egg-laying sites.

Seasonal abundance

Their numbers often rise in warm months when plant growth is high and nymphs can develop quickly.

Local specialization

Some species prefer dry prairies, some favor damp meadows, and others live along disturbed edges.

Ecological role

Small herbivores with large ripple effects

Grasshoppers convert plant tissue into insect biomass that feeds birds, reptiles, amphibians, spiders, and other insects. Their grazing can shape plant communities and recycle nutrients.

In some conditions, especially when weather favors survival and food is abundant, grasshopper populations can damage crops and rangeland. Knowing their life cycle helps land managers respond with better timing.

Field notes

How to observe grasshoppers well

  • Walk slowly along warm, grassy edges and watch for sudden jumps ahead of your feet.
  • Notice antenna length, wing length, hind-leg color, and whether the insect flies after jumping.
  • Listen for short buzzes, clicks, or snaps produced during movement or courtship displays.
  • Observe without handling when possible, because small insects lose water quickly in hot, dry weather.
